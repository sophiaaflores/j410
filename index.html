The Dorm Diaries

If you’re an incoming freshman at Cal Poly, figuring out housing can feel overwhelming as you prepare to leave for college. You’re asked to find a roommate, choose a living community, sort through different room types, and make sense of the costs all at once.

This website is built to help you understand your options more clearly by breaking down Cal Poly’s living communities using real data, student perspectives, and key details in order to make a confident choice about where you’ll call home.

To give you a more personal idea of what students have access to in their dorm buildings, here’s a look at how a first-year student living in the Red Bricks makes use of the spaces in her building.

April 2026 — Trinity Hall, Cal Poly San Luis Obispo. First-year journalism student Shayla Ibraham scans into her residence hall, where secured entry is part of daily life for on-campus housing.

April 2026 — Trinity Hall, Cal Poly San Luis Obispo. Ibraham enters the building where she lives, describing her first-year experience as positive. “Living in Trinity Hall has been fun, I really enjoy living with my friends,” she says.

April 2026 — Trinity Hall study lounge, Cal Poly San Luis Obispo. “The study lounge is definitely something I take advantage of, especially when I'm tired of being in my dorm.” Ibraham said.

As a first-year student at Cal Poly, you’ll live in one of six housing communities: yakʔitʸutʸu, Sierra Madre, Yosemite, South Mountain, North Mountain or Poly Canyon Village. Within each, you’ll be placed into a Residential Learning Community (RLC), where you’ll live alongside students who share similar interests, goals, or backgrounds.

“When choosing my RLC, I chose the one for my college, which was one of the best decisions I made because some of the people I know from class also happen to be my neighbors,” said Hannah Wegner, a first-year liberal studies major.

Cal Poly Dorm Living: A Student Perspective

Cal Poly is one of the few CSU campuses that requires first-year students to live on campus, contributing to a more residential experience.

“Living on campus places students in quick proximity to the classes, labs, clubs, resources and social connections to help them thrive at Cal Poly,” -Chris DeMoville, a public affairs representative for Cal Poly Housing.

In addition to housing, all first-year students are required to purchase a dining plan, which typically costs between $3,516 and $4,204 per year.
